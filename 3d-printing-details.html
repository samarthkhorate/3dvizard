Our 3D Printing Technologies

FDM (Fused Deposition Modeling)

How it works?

FDM melts and extrudes thermoplastic filament through a heated nozzle, layer by layer. It‚Äôs ideal for functional prototypes and low-cost parts using materials like PLA, ABS, and PETG.

Advantages

  • Cost-effective for prototypes
  • Wide range of thermoplastics (PLA, ABS)
  • Easy to use and maintain

Disadvantages

  • Lower surface quality
  • Weaker layer adhesion
  • Visible layer lines

SLA (Stereolithography)

How it works?

SLA uses a UV laser to cure liquid resin into hardened plastic, layer by layer. It produces high-detail, smooth surface parts.

Advantages

  • High-resolution prints
  • Smooth surface finish
  • Great for miniatures and dental models

Disadvantages

  • Resin is messy and toxic
  • Post-curing required
  • Fragile compared to FDM

SLS (Selective Laser Sintering)

How it works?

SLS fuses powdered nylon using a high-powered laser to build strong, durable parts. No support structures are needed.

Advantages

  • No support structures needed
  • Strong, functional parts
  • Complex geometries possible

Disadvantages

  • Rough surface finish
  • Expensive machines
  • Post-processing required

MJF (Multi Jet Fusion)

How it works?

MJF jets a binding agent onto nylon powder and fuses it using infrared energy, enabling fast production of functional end-use parts.

Advantages

  • Strong and functional parts
  • Fast production speeds
  • Uniform mechanical properties

Disadvantages

  • Expensive setup
  • Limited material options
  • Grey surface finish unless post-processed
